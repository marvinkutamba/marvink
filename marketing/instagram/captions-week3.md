An email came in last week that I wasn't expecting.

A client wrote to tell me she'd had to put her cat down. Unexpectedly. And she wanted me to know that the photos we'd taken together were something she would cherish forever.

I sat with that email for a while before I responded.

Because this client almost didn't book that session. She'd had a session with me a few years earlier for another cat who was sick and passed away shortly after. She knew what the photos would mean. But she still almost didn't get around to it.

She booked because a newsletter I sent reminded her.

A fortnightly email. That's all it was.

And I think about that a lot. Because if I'd stopped sending those newsletters, if I'd told myself "I don't want to bother people," she might not have had those photos.

That's why I show up consistently. Not to sell. To stay visible for the people who need what we offer, before it's too late.

This work matters. And so does making sure the right people can find you when they need you.

## Day 17 | Wednesday 25 March | Carousel
**Pillar:** Tactical Education
**Trigger:** Curiosity about tactics
**Stage:** Problem-aware / Solution-aware
**Visual:** Step-by-step carousel. Each slide = one stage of the journey. Clean, visual, easy to follow.

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
The enquiry-to-booking process most pet photographers skip.
(And why it's costing them clients who were genuinely ready to book.)

**Slide 2:**
Here's what most photographers do when an enquiry comes in:

Someone sends a message. The photographer replies with pricing. The person says "thanks, I'll think about it." And then... silence.

Sound familiar?

**Slide 3 - Stage 1: Warm the enquiry before pricing**
Before pricing means anything to someone, they need to feel like they want to work with you specifically.

That means understanding the experience, feeling connected, seeing themselves in your work.

If pricing is the first thing they receive, it has no context. Of course they're going to "think about it."

**Slide 4 - Stage 2: Ask before you answer**
When an enquiry comes in, resist the urge to immediately send your pricing guide.

Instead, ask a question. "Tell me a bit about your dog. What are they like?" or "What made you reach out now?"

This does two things: it starts a conversation, and it tells you if they're right-fit before you invest time in them.

**Slide 5 - Stage 3: The phone or video call**
A 15-20 minute consultation call is one of the highest-impact things you can do.

Not a hard sell. Just a conversation about their pet, what they're hoping to capture, and how a session works.

Members of our community consistently say: "If I can get them on the phone, I'll usually book them."

**Slide 6 - Stage 4: The follow-up**
If someone doesn't book right away, follow up. Warmly. Once or twice.

Not chasing. Just a genuine check-in: "Hi, I just wanted to see if you had any other questions after we chatted."

So many bookings happen in the follow-up that never would have happened otherwise.

**Slide 7 (CTA):**
You don't need to overhaul your whole process. Pick one stage and improve it this week.

If you don't like selling, it's probably because no one showed you how to do it from a place of service.

Most of us learned selling from the wrong examples. The pushy car salesperson. The telemarketer who won't take no for an answer. The high-pressure pitch that makes you feel like you're being talked into something.

So when it comes time to talk about our own work and our prices, we freeze. Or we discount before anyone even asks. Or we send the pricing guide and then never follow up because we don't want to be "that person."

But here's the thing: selling, when you genuinely believe in what you offer, is nothing like that.

It's guiding someone toward a decision that's good for them.

It's helping a client understand why a framed print on their wall will matter more in 10 years than a file on a hard drive.

It's following up warmly because you know that if they don't book now, they might not have those photos when they need them most.

That's not pushing. That's care.

You're not a salesperson. You're a guide.

You don't have to book more sessions to make more money.

It's the piece on their wall that makes them smile every single day.

That's not a file. That's a feeling. A connection. A legacy.

And clients who understand that don't ask for a discount. They ask when you're next available.

The shift isn't about working more. It's about offering something worth investing in.
