Consistency isn't posting every day.

It's having a rhythm you can actually maintain.

I see so many photographers burn themselves out trying to post every single day, and then crashing completely because it's not sustainable. They go all in for three weeks, then go quiet for two months, then feel guilty about it, then try again...

And round and round it goes.

That cycle isn't a discipline problem. It's a systems problem.

A sustainable rhythm looks different for everyone. For some people it's three posts a week with a fortnightly email. For others it's daily social but no email. The specifics matter less than whether you can actually keep it going, week after week, without it wrecking the rest of your life.

Simple beats ambitious. Every time.

**Slide 3 - Question 1:**
Where did your last 5 clients come from?

Write down the actual source. Not "Instagram" but specifically: was it a post, a reel, a DM? Was it a referral from whom? Was it a giveaway?

If the answer is "it would slow down significantly," you don't have a marketing system yet. You have a social media habit that requires constant feeding.

The lead sources that actually matter for pet photographers.

And a few that just eat your time.

After years of running my own studio and working with photographers in our community, here's what I see working consistently:

Word of mouth and referrals. Still the most reliable. But only works if you're creating experiences people actually want to talk about.

Google and organic search. Underrated and underused. A well-optimised website with real location-specific content keeps working for you while you sleep.

Email list. The most underutilised tool in every pet photographer's business. If you're not emailing your list regularly, you're leaving bookings on the table.

Instagram. Works, but only when it's connected to something. A lead magnet, an email list, a clear path from post to booking. On its own, it's just visibility.

And what doesn't work as well as people hope:

Charity auctions. They can bring in clients, but often the wrong ones. People who won a discounted session are not the same as people who understand the value of what you offer.

Facebook giveaways and competitions. Same problem. You attract a lot of people looking for free things, not a lot of people ready to invest in wall art.

None of this is absolute. Every business is different. But if you're pouring time into auctions and giveaways and wondering why the resulting clients aren't spending much, this might be why.

## Day 27 | Saturday 4 April | Static Post
**Pillar:** Credibility / Mindset Reframe
**Trigger:** Frustration with wrong-fit clients
**Stage:** Problem-aware
**Visual:** Ina, honest and direct. Or a clean text-based post. This is a story post, so the image should feel real.

---

Why I stopped running charity auctions.

For a while, auctions felt like a smart marketing move. Lots of visibility. New faces. A good cause.

But here's what I noticed: the clients who won an auction session almost never came back. And they almost never bought more than the minimum.

That's not a criticism of those people. But they weren't my ideal clients. They came because it was free or heavily discounted, not because they understood the value of what I offer.

And every session I spent with a wrong-fit client was a session I wasn't spending building the systems that would attract right-fit clients.

Now, that doesn't mean charity auctions are wrong for everyone. Some photographers use them brilliantly, especially when they're strategic about the minimum spend attached and the follow-up process after.

But if you're running auctions and wondering why the resulting clients are hard to upsell, it might be worth looking at whether you're attracting the right people in the first place.

Marketing that attracts everyone tends to convert no one at the level you want.

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
The sustainable business scan.
4 questions to check the health of your photography business right now.

**Slide 2:**
Most business problems don't appear overnight. They build slowly.

A dip in bookings. A creeping feeling that something's off. A sales average that hasn't moved in months.

The sustainable business scan helps you catch these things early, before a quiet month turns into a quiet quarter.

**Slide 3 - Question 1: Visibility**
Are new people finding you regularly?

Not just your existing followers. New people, who don't already know you. If all your recent enquiries are from people who already knew you in some way, your visibility is weak.

**Slide 4 - Question 2: Messaging**
When someone lands on your Instagram or your website, can they immediately understand what you offer and who it's for?

Ask a friend who doesn't know your business to look at your Instagram for 30 seconds. What do they think you do? Is that what you want them to think?

**Slide 5 - Question 3: Conversion**
When someone enquires, what happens next?

Do you have a consistent process? A consultation call? A follow-up sequence? Or does every enquiry feel like you're starting from scratch?

**Slide 6 - Question 4: Sustainability**
Could you keep doing what you're doing for the next 12 months without burning out?

If the answer is no, something needs to change. Not because you're failing, but because a business that works for 3 months and then needs recovery isn't a sustainable business.

**Slide 7 (CTA):**
One weak area means one thing to work on.

Not four. Not a complete overhaul. One.
