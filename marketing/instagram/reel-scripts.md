# Reel Scripts: 30-Day Instagram Strategy
**Brand:** Ina J Photography / Pet Photographers Collective
**Audience:** Pet photographers (coaching)
**Voice:** Warm, grounded, conversational. Written for natural speech, not polished copy.

---

## Notes for Filming

- These scripts are guides, not rigid text. If Ina goes off-script and comes back, that's fine.
- Speak naturally. Some filler words ("like", "you know") are on-brand when they're natural.
- Open warmly before the hook when possible. Even a breath or a look to camera before starting.
- No need to memorise word-for-word. Know the beats and speak from them.
- Keep energy warm throughout, not performative.
- Captions go in the caption field (see caption files). The script is the spoken content only.

## Script 1: "Empty Calendar" Scroll-Stopper
**Day 2 | Tuesday 10 March**
**Hook type:** Pattern interrupt, problem naming
**Target:** Unaware / Problem-aware, mid-stage avatar
**Length:** ~60-75 seconds

---

**[HOOK - on screen text: "If your calendar has more gaps than bookings right now..."]**

If your calendar has more gaps than bookings right now, I want you to stay with me for a second.

Because here's the thing. It's probably not your photography.

I've been doing this for years, and I talk to talented pet photographers every single week who are struggling to fill their calendars. And the most common thing I hear is some version of "I don't understand, I know I'm good, I just can't get consistent clients."

And I get it. Because that was me too.

Here's what I've figured out: there's a difference between being a good photographer and having a well-marketed photography business. And a lot of us are really good at the first one, but no one actually taught us the second.

So we post when we feel like it. We do a giveaway and hope it attracts the right people. We run a charity auction and wonder why those clients don't come back.

And then we blame ourselves. Like it's a "me" problem.

But it's not. It's a systems problem.

**[Transition: slow exhale or pause]**

And systems can be built.

If this sounds familiar, stick around. This week I'm talking about exactly this. And where to start.

**[Soft close to camera]**

## Script 2: "140 Sessions While Working Full-Time"
**Day 5 | Friday 13 March**
**Hook type:** Credibility, story-led
**Target:** Problem-aware / Solution-aware, all avatar stages
**Length:** ~75-90 seconds

---

**[HOOK - on screen text: "I did 140 sessions in one year while working full-time. Here's what I learned."]**

So, 140 sessions. In one year. While I was still working full-time for the government.

No VA. No help. Just me, juggling a day job, doing sessions on evenings and weekends, editing late at night.

I'm not telling you this to make it sound like some kind of achievement. Honestly? I was exhausted. I was running on adrenaline and probably not sleeping enough.

And the thing is, I thought volume was the answer. If I just did enough sessions, the money would follow. If I just stayed busy enough, the business would sort itself out.

But here's what I actually learned.

Volume is not the answer.

I was doing 140 sessions and not earning anywhere near what that number suggests I should have been. Because I hadn't sorted out what I was selling, who I was selling it to, or how to get clients who valued what I was offering.

**[Transition: "Here's the thing..."]**

Here's the thing. The photographers who work less and earn more, and they do exist, they're not doing more. They're doing smarter.

Fewer sessions. Clearer positioning. Higher averages. Clients who actually want what they offer.

That's not luck. That's a system.

So if you're in the thick of it right now, juggling everything, wondering when it gets easier... it gets easier when you stop trying to volume your way out of a positioning problem.

More on that later this week.

## Script 3: "Instagram Doesn't Need to Be Perfect, It Needs to Be Human"
**Day 9 | Tuesday 17 March**
**Hook type:** Permission, authenticity, vulnerability
**Target:** Unaware / Problem-aware
**Length:** ~60-75 seconds

---

**[HOOK - looks to camera, slightly informal, not perfectly lit]**

I'm going to be honest with you. I do not love how I look in a lot of my videos.

And for a while, that stopped me from posting as much as I should have. I'd film something, watch it back, cringe a bit, and then just... not post it.

And you know what happened when I wasn't posting? My reach dropped. My enquiries slowed down. And I was sitting there wondering why my business felt quiet.

Then I posted something I almost didn't post. The lighting was okay. I wasn't feeling confident that day. I kind of rambled a bit.

It made me $2,600 in bookings.

**[Pause. Genuine.]**

Not because it was perfect. Because it was real. Because I talked about something specific that the right person was also thinking about. And that person booked.

Your audience doesn't need you to be polished. They need you to be present.

They need to know there's a real human behind the photos. Someone who cares, who shows up, who will remember what they told you about their dog.

That's what builds trust. Not the perfect lighting.

So if you've been waiting until you feel "ready" to post...

This is me telling you: you're ready. Post the imperfect thing.

## Script 4: "The 3 Gaps Framework In Practice"
**Day 13 | Saturday 21 March**
**Hook type:** Framework education, practical application
**Target:** Problem-aware / Solution-aware
**Length:** ~75-90 seconds

---

**[HOOK - confident, direct, warm]**

Earlier this week I introduced the 3 gaps framework. Today I want to show you what it actually looks like when you apply it.

Because knowing the three gaps exist is one thing. Figuring out which one is yours, and what to do about it, is what actually moves the needle.

So. The three gaps are: visibility, message, and conversion.

**[Gap 1 - conversational, no rushing]**

Visibility is about whether new people are finding you. Not your existing followers. Not people who already know you. New people.

A quick way to check: where did your last three enquiries come from? If the honest answer is "mostly people I already know or who were referred by someone I know," your top-of-funnel is weak. You need more strangers finding you.

**[Gap 2]**

Message is about what happens when someone finds you. Do they immediately understand what you offer? Does your Instagram or website communicate the kind of experience you provide?

If people visit your profile and don't enquire, it's often because the message isn't clear enough. They don't know what the next step is. They can't quite imagine themselves as your client.

**[Gap 3]**

Conversion is about what happens after they reach out. If you're getting enquiries but not bookings, the gap is somewhere in your process. It could be how you respond. It could be pricing without context. It could be the absence of a follow-up.

**[Close, warm]**

So here's the question I want you to sit with: which of these three feels like your biggest gap right now?

You don't need to fix all three. Just identify the one that's causing the most damage, and start there.

## Script 5: "Why Your Enquiries Ghost After You Send Pricing"
**Day 16 | Tuesday 24 March**
**Hook type:** Problem naming, direct frustration address
**Target:** Problem-aware, mid-stage avatar
**Length:** ~75-90 seconds

---

**[HOOK - on screen text: "If your enquiries keep going quiet after pricing, watch this."]**

So you get an enquiry. You get excited. You respond with your pricing guide.

And then... nothing.

You wait a day. Two days. You follow up once. Still nothing. You're left wondering what went wrong, was it the price, was it something you said, did they find someone cheaper...

Sound familiar?

Here's what's actually happening in most cases.

It's not your price.

**[Pause]**

When someone reaches out to a photographer and the very first thing they receive is a pricing guide, they have no context for what they're looking at. They don't yet understand what the experience is like. They haven't connected with you as a person. They haven't pictured themselves at a session.

So they look at the number with no emotional investment, no context, no understanding of value. And they think "I'll think about it." Which almost always means no.

The fix isn't lowering your prices. The fix is what happens before pricing comes up.

**[Transition: "Here's the thing..."]**

Here's the thing. If you can have a five-minute conversation with someone before you send pricing, even a short back-and-forth over DM, you change everything. They're no longer looking at a number. They're thinking about working with you specifically.

The photographers in our community who say "if I can get them on the phone, I'll usually book them" are proof of this.

The phone call isn't the sales pitch. The phone call is the connection. And connection is what makes pricing feel worth it.

More on the enquiry process this week. If this landed for you, drop a comment.

## Script 6: "Artwork-Based Business vs Digitals-First Business"
**Day 20 | Saturday 28 March**
**Hook type:** Education, reframe, practical
**Target:** Problem-aware / Solution-aware, mid and established stages
**Length:** ~90 seconds

---

**[HOOK - direct, clear, confident but warm]**

I want to talk about the difference between a digitals-first business and an artwork-based business. Because the shift between the two isn't just about what you sell. It's about what clients expect before they even contact you.

In a digitals-first business, clients come in expecting a file. They might upgrade to a framed print or an album if you present it beautifully during the reveal. But their default expectation is digital.

In an artwork-based business, clients come in already expecting a piece on their wall. That expectation was set by your marketing before they ever enquired.

**[Key distinction]**

And that's the key difference. Not the product. The expectation.

Which means the shift doesn't start at the sales session. It starts at your Instagram. Your website. The language you use. The examples you show. The stories you tell about past clients and what they walked away with.

If your content mostly shows beautiful photos and not much else, people will come to you for beautiful photos. That's what they'll expect.

But if your content shows wall art in real homes, clients holding framed prints, the reveal moment, the before and after of an empty wall... people come in already picturing their dog on their wall.

**[Practical takeaway]**

So here's the question worth asking: what expectation is your current marketing setting?

If digital-only clients keep showing up, it might not be a sales problem. It might be a messaging problem. Your content is attracting people who see photos, not people who see artwork.

The shift doesn't require a complete overhaul. Start with the content you're showing and the language you're using around it.

Your work is good. Let's make sure your marketing is doing it justice.

## Script 7: "A Simple Weekly Marketing Rhythm"
**Day 23 | Tuesday 31 March**
**Hook type:** Tactical, practical, permission-giving
**Target:** Problem-aware / Solution-aware, all stages
**Length:** ~75-90 seconds

---

**[HOOK - on screen text: "A simple weekly marketing rhythm that doesn't require more time."]**

I want to give you something really practical today. A weekly marketing rhythm that you can actually maintain, even if you have a day job, kids, editing backlogs, or all three happening at once.

Because I've noticed something in our community. The photographers who consistently fill their calendars aren't necessarily doing more marketing than everyone else. They just have a rhythm they keep showing up for. Simple. Repeatable. Boring, almost.

So here's what a basic weekly rhythm could look like.

**[Walk through casually, like you're thinking it out]**

Monday: plan your content for the week. Even if that's just a voice memo to yourself about what you want to say. Five minutes.

Tuesday or Wednesday: one post. Could be a reel, could be a static, could be a carousel. Something that shows you're here, that you have something to say.

Thursday: engage. Respond to comments. Reply to DMs. Leave thoughtful comments on other pet photographers' posts. Community is also marketing.

Friday: email your list. Even just a short one. One idea, one story, one thing they can do this week.

Repeat.

**[Pause]**

That's it. That's not a full marketing strategy. But it's a rhythm. And a rhythm you can maintain is worth a hundred complex strategies you abandon after two weeks.

You might be thinking "that seems too simple." Good. Simple is the point.

What's the part of this rhythm you've been most inconsistent with? Drop it in the comments. I'm curious.

## Script 8: "What I'd Tell Myself In Year One"
**Day 26 | Friday 3 April**
**Hook type:** Story, credibility, legacy
**Target:** Unaware / Problem-aware, all avatar stages
**Length:** ~90 seconds

---

**[HOOK - reflective, warm, not performative]**

If I could go back and have a conversation with myself in year one of my pet photography business, there are a few things I'd want to say.

Not because they would have made everything easier. Some of the hard things were important. But because they would have saved me a lot of time going in the wrong direction.

**[Beat 1]**

First: the business side matters as much as the photography side. And I know that can feel uncomfortable if you got into this because you love photography. I did too. But a business that doesn't work financially isn't giving you the freedom to keep doing the thing you love.

The sooner you make friends with the business side, the easier the whole thing gets.

**[Beat 2]**

Second: you don't need more clients. You need better-positioned clients. I spent so long trying to book more, more sessions, more people, more volume. And I was exhausted. The shift that changed everything was getting clearer on who I was for, and making my marketing speak to that person specifically.

**[Beat 3]**

Third: show up even when it feels pointless. The newsletter I send every fortnight, the posts I make even when I think no one is paying attention, that is what keeps you visible for people who need you. I have had clients book because of an email I sent months earlier. You never know who's watching.

**[Close, direct, warm]**

And I guess the last thing I'd say is: your work is good. That's not the thing holding you back.

It's the systems and the strategy around it. And those can be built.
