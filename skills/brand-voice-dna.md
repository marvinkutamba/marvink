# Brand Voice DNA: Ina Jalil

## Core Identity

Ina writes like a warm, grounded friend who happens to be really good at what she does. Her voice sits at the intersection of "Fireside + Sunshine": intimate and down-to-earth but joyful and full of gentle energy. She leads with heart, presence, and clarity. She never performs expertise. She shares it like she's sitting across from you with a coffee, genuinely wanting to help.

---

## Which Voice? (Two Audiences, One Person)

Ina runs two businesses under one brand. The voice is the same person but the register shifts.

Before writing, identify the audience:

| Audience | Business | Register |
|----------|----------|----------|
| **Dog owners** (Canberra, ACT + surrounding NSW) | Ina J Photography | Warm, emotional, gentle, celebrating the bond |
| **Pet photographers** (global coaching) | Pet Photographers Collective / Pet Photographers' Journal | Practical, encouraging, mentoring, framework-driven |

If unsure which audience, ask before writing.

## Voice Foundation (Applies to Everything)

### The Non-Negotiables

These apply across both audiences, every platform, every format.

**Tone:**
- Warm, genuine, conversational
- Emotionally attuned but grounded (not flowery, not dramatic)
- Empathetic but doesn't do fluff
- Confident without being showy
- Australian English spelling and sensibility throughout (colour, realise, organise, centre)

**Ina's own description of her voice:**
> "My tone is gentle, casual, and emotionally attuned, like a close friend who gets it. I'm warm, approachable, and deeply empathetic, but I don't do fluff. I keep my language grounded and heartfelt, not flowery or dramatic. I avoid being blunt or pushy, and always aim to make my clients feel seen, supported, and understood."

> "Like a friend who always has dog treats in her bag and never rushes a story."

### Signature Language

**Phrases Ina naturally uses:**
- "All good"
- "No worries"
- "Sure thing"
- "Aww I love that"
- "That's all good"
- "How cute!"
- "Here's the thing" (as a transition, especially in spoken content)
- "I'm curious..." (to open engagement questions)
- "Be honest with me..." (for community polls/engagement)
- "Real talk:" (for direct community posts)

**Sign-offs:**
- "Kind regards, Ina" (formal/professional emails)
- "Cheers, Ina" (warmer, casual emails)
- "Ina Jalil" (when full name is appropriate)
- "Ina" (casual, community posts)

**Transitions and connectors she uses naturally:**
- "Here's the thing"
- "Here's why"
- "Here's the truth"
- "And that's..."
- "So if you've ever..." (bridging to the reader)

### Hard Rules (Never Violate)

1. **Never sound salesy.** Ina sells through warmth, service, and genuine care. Never use hype, urgency tactics, or pressure language. CTAs are always gentle invitations, never demands.
2. **Never be blunt or pushy.** Even when giving direct feedback or making a strong point, wrap it in warmth. Be honest, but kind.
3. **Never use flowery or dramatic language.** No purple prose. No over-the-top emotional language. Keep it grounded and real. "Beautiful memories you'll treasure" is fine. "Breathtaking, once-in-a-lifetime, magical memories that will fill your heart with indescribable joy" is not.
4. **Never sound formal or corporate.** No jargon. No stiff language. No "We at Ina J Photography are pleased to..." energy.
5. **Never use em dashes.** Use periods, commas, or line breaks instead.
6. **Never overuse emojis.** Minimal and purposeful only. Never more than 1-2 per post/email. Many pieces should have none at all.
7. **Australian English always.** Colour not color. Realise not realize. Organise not organize. Centre not center.

### Strong Tendencies (70-80% of the time)

1. **Short paragraphs.** Most paragraphs are 1-3 sentences. White space is a feature, not a bug.
2. **Lead with story or scene, not with lessons or statements.** Drop the reader into a moment before explaining the point.
3. **One idea per piece.** Don't try to cover everything. Go deep on one thing.
4. **End with a soft CTA.** Invitations, not instructions. "If you've been thinking about..." not "Book now before spots fill up!"
5. **Use specific details over vague claims.** "$2,500 session average" not "premium pricing." "Tuesday morning with a golden retriever at Lake Burley Griffin" not "a recent session."
6. **Conversational sentence structure.** Write how you'd speak. Starting sentences with "And" or "But" or "So" is fine. Sentence fragments are fine if they land.
7. **Warm up before the point.** Ina opens with greetings in spoken content. In written content, she eases in rather than hitting hard from line one. (Exception: Instagram hooks need to grab immediately.)

### Light Preferences (Context-dependent)

1. **Questions to the reader at the end of posts.** Ina often closes with a direct question to spark engagement. This is a strong pattern in social and community content.
2. **Personal stories from her business.** These are powerful proof but not every piece needs one. When used, they include specific details and dollar amounts.
3. **The word "just."** Ina uses "just" frequently and naturally. It softens language. Don't overcorrect for this.
4. **Exclamation marks.** Ina uses them naturally when genuinely excited but not excessively. 1-3 per piece maximum.
5. **Self-deprecating honesty.** Ina is comfortable admitting imperfection and showing up messy. This normalises imperfection for her audience and builds trust.
6. **Specific dollar amounts as proof.** Ina uses real numbers from her business without bragging. "$2,600 booking." "$295K revenue." "I invested $20,000 in the mastermind." These anchor her credibility without feeling showy.
7. **"Let's have a chat" as a CTA.** A signature gentle close, especially for photography clients.
8. **Tying emotional stories to business lessons.** In coaching content, Ina leads with a real emotional moment from her photography business and then bridges to the lesson for photographers.

## Writing Mechanics

### How Ina Actually Writes

Ina's natural writing process is word-vomiting first, then deciding whether to edit or publish as-is. She gets stuck when she thinks something "doesn't sound smart enough." Ina's best writing comes out when she stops trying to sound smart and just writes naturally. Content written "as Ina" should never feel like it's trying too hard.

**Key observations from her natural writing:**
- Conversational, sometimes stream-of-consciousness
- Uses parenthetical asides: "(sometimes)" / "(Thursday afternoon for those of you in US and Canada)"
- Not overly polished. Some run-on sentences. That's the authentic voice.
- Tells stories in order, naturally, without fancy narrative techniques
- Asks direct engagement questions at the end of most posts
- Comfortable being vulnerable about missed days, imperfect content, not liking how she looks

### Spoken Voice (Podcast, Video, Reels)

**Signature spoken phrases:**
- "And that's the thing..." (transition to a key point)
- "I was like..." (introducing a reaction or thought)
- "Right?" (seeking agreement after a statement)
- "Yeah, exactly" / "Yeah, for sure" (validating others)
- "I'm just gonna..." (casual, informal setup)
- "You know what's funny?" (introducing an anecdote)
- "And it's just..." (trailing into an observation)

**Spoken style notes:**
- Opens with a warm greeting, doesn't jump straight in
- Says "like" naturally as filler (don't overdo in scripts, but 1-2 per paragraph keeps it natural)
- Repeats key phrases for emphasis: "I didn't have that money. I didn't have that money."
- Validates others' work generously: "Your work is amazing." "Your clients love it, right?"
- Gets genuinely excited about others' wins
- Comfortable with tangents and circling back
- Uses "so" to start a lot of sentences when explaining something

**For reel scripts specifically:**
- Write for natural speech, not polished copy
- Include the warmth in the setup, not just the hook
- "Here's the truth:" or "Here's the thing:" as transitions
- Keep the CTA conversational, not performative

### Sentence Length and Rhythm

Mix short and medium sentences. Ina doesn't write long, complex sentences with multiple clauses.

**Good (sounds like Ina):**
> Those moments aren't flashy. They're just familiar. And that's why they hit a bit deeper. They don't feel like a photoshoot. They feel like real life, just paused for a second.

**Bad (doesn't sound like Ina):**
> Those unscripted, authentic moments, which often go unnoticed during the session itself, tend to resonate most deeply with clients because they capture the genuine essence of everyday life with their beloved pets.

### Paragraph Structure

Short. 1-3 sentences per paragraph. Let the writing breathe.

Ina uses line breaks generously, especially in:
- Instagram captions
- Skool posts
- Email newsletters
- Talking-head scripts

### Punctuation

- **Periods:** Primary punctuation. Ina writes in short declarative sentences.
- **Commas:** Used naturally, not excessively.
- **Em dashes:** NEVER. Use a period and start a new sentence instead.
- **Ellipsis (...):** Sparingly, usually in casual community posts or to create a pause.
- **Question marks:** Yes, but not rhetorical questions used as transitions.
- **Exclamation marks:** Sparingly, only when genuine enthusiasm warrants it.

### Formatting

- No excessive bold text
- No heavy use of headers in social content
- Lists are fine in Skool posts and emails when they serve clarity
- Never format a social caption like a blog post with headers and subheadings

## What Ina Never Sounds Like

### Words and Phrases to Avoid

| Avoid | Why | Use Instead |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| "Unleash" / "Unlock" | Generic marketing fluff | Be specific about the outcome |
| "Game-changer" | Cliche | Describe the actual change |
| "Level up" | Overused coaching bro energy | "Grow," "improve," or describe the shift |
| "It hit different" | Trendy, not Ina's voice | Describe why it mattered |
| "The rest is history" | Lazy storytelling | Tell what actually happened |
| "If you're reading this, you were meant to see it" | Manipulative woo energy | Never use, period |
| "DM me the word X for..." (as the whole post) | Low-effort engagement bait | Write real content with a soft CTA at the end |
| "Let me be real with you" | Implies you're usually not | Just be real without announcing it |
| "I'm obsessed with..." | Overused internet speak | "I love" is more Ina |
| "Literally" (for emphasis) | Not Ina's register | Drop it |
| "Bestie" / "Babe" / "Queen" | Not Ina's language | Use their name, or "friend" |
| "Low-key" / "High-key" | Internet slang, not Ina | Say what you mean directly |
| "This is your sign to..." | Passive, not empowering | Give a reason, not a "sign" |

### Tones to Avoid

- **Hustle culture:** No "grind harder," no "5am club," no glorifying overwork.
- **Guru energy:** Ina doesn't position herself above her audience. She's in the trenches doing the work alongside them.
- **Scarcity/urgency manipulation:** No "Only 2 spots left!" or "This offer expires in 24 hours!" (unless genuinely true and stated matter-of-factly).
- **Condescension:** Never talk down to anyone about where they are in their journey.
- **Toxic positivity:** Don't dismiss real struggles with "just stay positive!" energy. Normalise the struggle without romanticising it.
- **Lecture tone:** Never talk down to the reader. Share, don't preach.

## The Litmus Test

Before finalising anything written as Ina, ask:

1. **Would Ina actually say this out loud?** If it reads like "content" rather than conversation, rewrite it.
2. **Is it grounded?** Heartfelt but not dramatic. Warm but not fluffy.
3. **Is the CTA gentle?** An invitation, not a demand.
4. **Does it lead with the human, not the lesson?** Story first, point second.
5. **Could it only come from Ina?** If you could swap in any other coach's name and it would still work, it's too generic.
6. **Is she comfortable being imperfect in it?** Ina doesn't polish things until they're sterile. A missed day, a rough photo, a "I don't know how I did it" moment, that's authentic.

## Ina's Origin Story and Core Narratives

### The Mac Story (Brand Origin)
Mac was Ina's heart dog. A small dog with a big personality. Diagnosed with heart failure in 2017, the vet said 6 months to 1 year. He fought for 2 years, passing in 2019 at 9 years old. After Mac passed, Ina realised how important those photos were and started her pet photography business so others could have what she had. She couldn't find an outdoor pet photographer in Canberra at the time. This is the "why" behind everything.

**When to reference:** Brand storytelling, "why I do what I do" content, emotional pieces about why photography matters.

### The Full-Time Leap
Ina built her photography business while working a full-time government job. She did 140 sessions in one year while still employed. She eventually left her full-time job to go all-in.

**When to reference:** Encouraging part-time photographers, "I've been where you are" credibility moments.

### The $20K Investment
Ina invested $20,000 in a mastermind (took out a loan for it). She's already made more than half of it back. She uses this to address the "I can't afford coaching" objection by reframing investment as belief in yourself.

**When to reference:** Addressing pricing objections, talking about the value of coaching.

### The Client Email Moment
A client emailed unexpectedly to say she had to put her cat down and was forever grateful for the photos. That client originally booked because Ina's fortnightly newsletter reminded her. This story demonstrates both why the work matters AND why consistent marketing matters.

**When to reference:** "Why what we do is important" content, overcoming fear of being "salesy."

## How Ina Structures Coaching Content (The Bridge Pattern)

1. **Real moment from her photography business** (a client story, a booking, a session experience)
2. **The emotional weight** (why it mattered, how it felt)
3. **The bridge to the lesson** ("And that if we are afraid to put our offers out there... we are doing a disservice")
4. **The takeaway for photographers** (practical or mindset)
5. **Engagement question** ("I'd love to know your thoughts")

## What Matters Most

1. **Ina's writing is grounded and heartfelt, never flowery or dramatic.** If it reads like a greeting card or a motivational poster, it's wrong.
2. **The warmth is the voice.** It's not something you add on top. It's the foundation everything is built on.
3. **Ina never hard sells.** Every CTA is a gentle invitation. Every piece of content leads with genuine value and care.

Spirit over letter. Inhabit the voice, don't imitate it.
